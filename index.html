---SMALL FACTS---

Sea turtles outlived the dinosaurs

Sea turtles evolved 110 million years ago and have only little changes during a long period of time.

Starfish is not a fish

In fact, it's an echinoderm, closely related to sea urchins and sand dollars.

Jellyfish are not made up of jelly

Jellyfish are mostly made up of water, about 95%.

Blue whales have big hearts

Blue whales have the biggest heart in the animal kingdom, weighing about 400 pounds (180 kilograms). It is the same weight of a gorilla, and as big as a bumper car.

Octopuses have blue blood

To survive in the deep ocean, octopuses evolved a copper rather than iron-based blood called hemocyanin, which turns its blood blue.

Sharks are boneless

Sharks have cartilaginous skeletons. Cartilage is less dense than bone, allowing sharks to move quickly through the water without using too much energy.

Squid are intelligent animals

They have well-developed nervous systems and are capable of complex behaviours.

Crabs can see all around them

Crabs have eyes on their stalks which are attached to their heads and let them see all around, 360 degrees.

Pufferfish is poisonous

The Puffer is harmless, unless eaten. The liver, intestines, gonads and skin are highly poisonous and cause death in around 60% of people who eat it.

Clams are known to be sequential hermaphrodites

They can change their gender throughout their lifespan. They may start as males and transition to females as they get older.

Atlantic salmon

Atlantic salmon live in coastal seas and feed on pelagic invertebrates and some fishes. Salmon also face many other threats that affect their survival, such as poor water quality, degraded freshwater habitats from land use practices, fish diseases, predation from introduced and invasive species, and interbreeding with escaped fish raised on farms for commercial aquaculture.

Starhorn Coral

Staghorn coral is one of the most important corals in the Caribbean. Staghorn coral can form dense groups called “thickets” in very shallow water. These provide important habitats for other reef animals, especially fish. The greatest threat to staghorn coral is ocean warming, which causes the corals to release the algae that live in their tissue and provide them food, usually causing death. Other threats to staghorn coral are ocean acidification, land-based sources of pollution, and low nutrient waters in which they thrive.

Polar Bear

The polar bear is the largest and most powerful carnivore on land, a title it shares with a subspecies of brown bear called the Kodiak bear. It has no natural predators and knows no fear of humans, making it an extremely dangerous animal. Polar bears usually swim underwater at depths of only about 3-4.5 m (9.8-14.8 ft.). They can remain submerged for more than one minute. Maximum dive duration is unknown; however, the longest polar bear dive observed to date lasted a total of 3 minutes and 10 seconds covering a distance of 45 to 50 m (148–164 ft.) without surfacing.

Clown Fish

Clownfish live on coastal seaward reefs and in shallow and sheltered lagoons at depths of 1-12 m (3.3-39.3 ft). Clownfish are protandrous hermaphrodites, meaning that they all mature as males but can change their sex to female. These fish aerate the water around the tentacle of their host anemone, remove waste from its oral disc, and provide nitrogenous waste that the anemone uses as food. Contrary to popular belief, they do not capture food for the anemone.

Blue tang

The blue tang may cause ciguatera poisoning if consumed by humans. Tangs are found in coral reefs and coastal grassy or rocky areas at depths of 6-131 feet (2-40 m). In coral reefs, blue tangs live in holes and crevices where they are sheltered from predators while they sleep at night.

Leafy Seadragon

Named seadragons after the dragons of Chinese legends, leafy seadragons resemble the dragons of fairy tales. Leafy seadragons can be found from the surface to depths of 98 feet (30 metres). Shallow water leafy seadragons are yellow-brown or greenish with dark pink rib bands and green-brown pronounced leafy appendages. Those that live in deeper water are dark brown to burgundy red.

Killer Whale

The Killer whale (Orcinus orca) is a toothed whale belonging to the oceanic dolphin family. It is the largest member of this family. Killer whales are highly social animals; they are active during the day and sleep at night. Killer whales are apex predators and are at the top of the food chain. They prey upon a wide range of large prey including seals, smaller whales and dolphins, sea lions, different fish, sharks, squid, octopi, sea birds, sea turtles, sea otters, and river otters. Researchers theorize that some killer whales may have a maximum diving depth ranging from 258 m to 452 m (846 ft. to 1,483 ft.).

Sea Lion

Sea Lions prefer sandy beaches, usually in isolated bays and sheltered areas. Sea lions can remain underwater for an average of 8 to 20 minutes. Sea lions can dive to depths between 450 and 900 feet (135 - 272 m). Sea Lion's diet is poorly known but probably includes a variety of fish and invertebrates such as octopus and squid. There is some suggestion that they occasionally take penguins.

Bull Shark

The Bull Shark can live in a wide range of habitats from coastal marine and estuarine to freshwater. It has been recorded from the surf zone down to a depth of at least 150 m. It is the only species of shark that is known to stay for extended periods in freshwater. Bull Sharks have an omnivorous diet that includes fishes (including other sharks), dolphins, turtles, birds, mollusks, echinoderms, and even terrestrial mammals.

Great White Shark

The great white shark, or the great white, can be up to 6 metres long and weights about 3 tonnes. Its life span is over 70 years, which makes it one of the longest-living cartilage fish. It’s also one of the fastest sharks. Great white sharks are predators that feed on fish (mainly tuna), other sharks, rays, marine mammals (cetaceans, seals, otters), sea turtles, and birds. In the open ocean, great white sharks have also been recorded at depths of up to 1,200m

Bottlenose Dolphin

Bottlenose dolphins are found in temperate and tropical waters around the world. They inhabit a wide variety of habitats, including harbors, bays, gulfs, and estuaries, as well as nearshore coastal waters, deeper waters over the continental shelf, and even far offshore in the open ocean. They are highly intelligent species and use sound both for communication and to hunt for food. The deepest dive ever recorded for a bottlenose dolphin was 300 meters (990 feet).

Human

On September 18, 2014, Ahmed Gabr broke all diving records by plunging to a depth of 1,090 feet (332 metres) beneath the waters of the Red Sea, in Egypt. It took 15 minutes to go down and nearly fourteen hours to resurface: an endeavor testing the limits of human endurance.

Sea angle

Sea angels are found worldwide in cold and temperate waters. They typically live in the midwater zone, from the surface to 2,000 feet deep (600 meters). Though their name implies an angelic nature, sea angels are, in fact, predators with quite a bite. Sea angels have the remarkable ability to switch genders. Specifically, they are protandrous hermaphrodites, which means they start male and turn female throughout their lives.

King Crab

Amongst king crab species, the scarlet king crab is comparatively smaller. Found in depths from intertidal to 183 meters (600 ft). Red king crabs can only grow by molting (shedding their old shell and growing a new one). After molting they are soft and vulnerable to predators until their new shell hardens. Red king crabs eat almost anything they can find and crush with their claws.

Giant Pacific Octopus

Giant Pacific octopuses can be found more than 330 feet (100 m) underwater. Since they lack a protective outer shell, they use their camouflage abilities to stay safe. When threatened, octopuses can also cloud predators in black ink. The ink is toxic and can be deadly to octopuses if confined to a small space with little current flow. Giant Pacific octopuses spend most of their lives alone. They hunt at night for shrimp, clams, lobsters, and fish, but have also been known to eat small sharks using their beak-like mouths to puncture prey.

Sperm Whale Dive

Sperm whales hunt for food during deep dives that routinely reach depths of 2,000 feet and can last for 45 minutes. They are capable of diving to depths of over 10,000 feet for over 60 minutes. After long, deep dives, individuals come to the surface to breathe and recover for several minutes before initiating their next dive. Because sperm whales spend most of their time in deep waters, their diet consists of species such as squid, sharks, skates, and fish that also occupy deep ocean waters.

Big red Jellyfish

They live at depths of 650 to 1,500 meters (2,000 to 4,800 feet). Big Red Jellyfish is a filter feeder, which means it captures and consumes small organisms suspended in the water. They possess long, slender tentacles that extend below their bell, allowing them to capture prey efficiently. Their tentacles are equipped with numerous stinging cells called cnidocytes, which contain nematocysts, specialized structures used to immobilize prey.

Telescope Octopus

The Telescope Octopus is a mysterious, deep sea octopus found at depths between 150 and 2,000 metres (500 - 6,500 ft) in tropical and subtropical Indo-Pacific waters. Their transparent, gelatinous flesh ensures they leave no shadow in the gloomy twilight to betray their presence to predators that may lurk below them. There are only two body parts they can't make invisible: their digestive gland and their eyes. Their utterly bizarre eyes.

Colossal squid

The colossal squid is a massive squid that lives in the deep sea surrounding Antarctica, and it is the holder of multiple records. Not only is it the largest invertebrate on Earth, but it also has the largest eyes of any animal, larger even than those of the great whales. Giant squid lives deep underwater—in the Twilight Zone—at depths between 1,000 feet and about 2,000 feet.

Titanic Wreckage

The Royal Mail Ship Titanic was thought to be unsinkable. But on April 14, 1912, during her maiden voyage, Titanic struck an iceberg in the North Atlantic. The wreck of the Titanic lies at a depth of about 12,500 feet (3,800 metres; 2,100 fathoms), about 370 nautical miles (690 kilometres) south-southeast off the coast of Newfoundland.

Fangtooth

It has been found as deep as 5,000 meters (16,404 feet), making it one of the deepest living fish, but is most common between 500 and 2,000 meters (1,640 and 6,562 feet). During the day it stays in deeper areas of the ocean and at night, migrates up to shallower water to feed. Younger, smaller fangtooth fish filter zooplankton from the water, and adults feed on fish and squid.

Comb Jelly

Found at a depth of 7,217 metres (23,700 ft), it is the deepest dwelling ctenophore discovered. The comb jelly is a beautiful, oval-shaped animal with eight rows of tiny comblike plates that it beats to move itself through the water. As it swims, the comb rows break up (diffract) light to produce a shimmering rainbow effect.
